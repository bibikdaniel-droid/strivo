Frequently asked questions

Everything you need to know about Strivo.

Spreadsheets and Jira boards are general-purpose tools that require someone to maintain them manually. Strivo is purpose-built for AI tool governance: it structures the registry around tools, owners, and approval states, routes sign-off requests to the right stakeholders automatically, tracks the full approval history, and flags cost anomalies and duplicate tools. Spreadsheets go stale. Strivo is designed so they do not.

Today, Strivo is a registry your team fills in. We built it this way intentionally: the act of registering a tool forces the right questions upfront. Who owns it, what data does it touch, has it been approved. Auto-discovery is on the roadmap. The plan is to connect to SSO providers, expense platforms, and SaaS management tools to surface tools that are already running without going through the registry. That becomes the enforcement layer on top of the manual foundation.

When a team submits a tool for approval, Strivo routes the request to the designated reviewers: typically finance, IT, or compliance depending on the tool type. Reviewers get a structured request with the tool details, vendor, data access scope, and cost. They approve, request changes, or reject, and every decision is logged with a timestamp. The tool moves through a defined lifecycle: Draft, In Review, Approved, Active, and eventually Retired. Nothing is verbal. Nothing lives in a Slack thread.

This is the most common starting point. Strivo supports bulk registration so you can bring existing tools into the registry quickly, assign owners, and flag which ones have never had a formal approval. From there, your team works through the backlog at whatever pace makes sense, prioritizing tools that touch sensitive data or have the highest spend. You do not need to be starting from zero. Most founding teams use Strivo to get a baseline picture of what is already running before they start enforcing anything.

In the current version, Strivo does not require access to your internal systems to function. Teams submit tool information manually: the vendor, team, owner, cost, and data scope. Integrations with SSO providers, expense tools, and AI vendors are planned for later phases. When those integrations ship, they will be opt-in and scoped to the minimum data needed. Strivo is designed with the assumption that it will eventually hold sensitive information about your AI stack, so security decisions like append-only audit logs and workspace isolation are being built in from the start.

Strivo is designed around four roles. Team members who adopt AI tools submit them for registration and approval. Owners are accountable for maintaining tool records and cost. Reviewers, typically finance, IT, or compliance, are the stakeholders who sign off on new tools. Leadership, including CTOs, AI leads, and COOs, use the dashboard to see what is running across the company. In practice, most teams start with one person owning setup and expand access as the registry fills in.

The current focus is a solid registry and approval engine: tool submission, owner assignment, approval routing, review queue, and spend tracking. Phase 2 brings duplicate detection at submission, renewal reminders, an internal approved-tools catalog teams can browse before purchasing, CSV compliance exports, and a Slack bot for approvals without leaving chat. The longer-term vision is auto-discovery: Strivo connecting to SSO, expense platforms, and browser activity to surface AI tools automatically and turn the product from a manual registry into a live control layer for your entire AI stack.
